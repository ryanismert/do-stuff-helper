# User Profile Interview Guide

Reference for the user-profile-builder skill. Adapted from the 8760 Hours methodology by Alex Vermeer. This document is read by the AI interviewer at interview time — it is not shown to the user directly.

---

## The Twelve Life Areas

Each area represents a component of a whole life. Aspects of life naturally overlap between areas — classify things where they feel most primary.

1. **Values & Purpose** — Your deeper, underlying, fundamental values and wants. Your philosophy of life. Your sense of purpose, vision, and meaning.

2. **Contribution & Impact** — How you give value to the world, make a difference, and have a positive impact.

3. **Location & Tangibles** — Your physical presence in the world. Where you are. Your living situation. Your stuff, what you own and why. Your material sufficiency. Your mobility.

4. **Money & Finances** — Your savings, investments, assets, and debt. How your money is organized and managed. Your inflows, budgets, and outflows.

5. **Career & Work** — Your work, job, career, and business. Your position, title, role, and responsibilities. Your source of income.

6. **Health & Fitness** — Your eating habits, diet, exercise habits, and activity levels. How resilient you are to sickness. Your overall energy level. Your sleeping patterns and quality. Your major health issues and susceptibilities.

7. **Education & Skill Development** — Your learning, education, and mental development. Your talents and skills. Your skill development, practicing, and training.

8. **Social Life & Relationships** — The intimate relationship(s) you have or want to have. The quality of your relationships. Your home life and relationships with family members. Your friend circles and social experiences. Your club, organization, and community memberships.

9. **Emotions & Well-Being** — Your general feeling about life, optimism or pessimism, positivity or negativity. Your emotional intelligence. Your subjective well-being, self-esteem, self-respect, and self-compassion.

10. **Character & Identity** — Your identity and model of yourself. Your strengths and weaknesses, virtues and vices. Your intelligence, integrity, honesty, courage, compassion, honor, self-discipline, and so on.

11. **Productivity & Organization** — Your memorized solutions, daily routine, schedule, effectiveness, organization, and productivity. Your setup, systems, processes, tools, and techniques.

12. **Adventure & Creativity** — Your hobbies, pastimes, and things you do for fun and adventure. Your creativity and its expression.

## Phase 1: Initial Overview Questions

Start here to warm up the user's thinking. These are broad, backward-looking questions about the past year (or recent period). Spend 5-10 minutes. The goal is to get the "obvious" things out of their head quickly before diving deep.

### Backward-Looking

1. What went well?
2. What did not go well?
3. Where did you try hard?
4. Where did you not try hard enough?

### Current Situation (right now)

5. What is the current situation in your life overall?
6. How do things stand right now?
7. How would you summarize the status of your life to a friend?

### Interviewer Notes

- Do not press for exhaustive answers here. The user should aim for roughly ten items across these questions.
- Capture responses as bullet points. These feed into the profile's `overview` section.
- Be objective and descriptive. If the user starts making judgments or plans, note them separately and redirect to the descriptive picture.

## Phase 2: Per-Area Present Reality

For each of the twelve life areas, conduct a deep dive. Use the general prompts first, then the area-specific questions as follow-up probes. Not every question needs to be asked — use them to draw out detail when the user's initial response is thin.

### General Prompts (use for every area)

- How would you describe the current state of this area?
- A short summary of your thoughts about this area of your life.
- Any project status updates relevant to this area?
- Projects completed in the past year?
- Milestones from the past year?
- Important events from the past year?
- Accomplishments?
- Failures?
- Key life metrics relevant to this area (income, weight, relationship quality, donation amount, etc.)?
- Strengths in this area? Weaknesses?

After discussing the area, ask for a **rating (1-7)** and the **Hamming questions** (see below).

### Area-Specific Questions

#### Values & Purpose
- Do you have a sense of purpose and direction in life?
- What do you want out of life?
- How do you want the world to be different?
- What is your philosophy of life?
- What are your fundamental values?
- What do you truly value?

#### Contribution & Impact
- How are you giving value to the world? How much value?
- How much money did you give away in the past year? Who did it go to? Did it have the most possible impact?
- Are you taking responsibility for, and reducing, your environmental impact on the world?
- Are you making a difference?
- Are you contributing to important and worthy causes that you care about?
- How much impact does your existence have (environmentally, socially, cognitively)?

#### Location & Tangibles
- Do you have material sufficiency?
- Where do you live? Where is your home? Where in the world are you?
- What is your living situation like?
- Are you tied to one location? Are you readily mobile (if desired)?
- How much stuff do you own? Do you have too many things?
- Is your life cluttered?

#### Money & Finances
- Do you have savings? How much? Where are they?
- Do you have an emergency fund? How much? Where is it?
- What is the status of your assets and investments?
- Do you know where your money goes?
- Do you budget? What is your budget?
- Are you aware of how you spend your money?
- How do you want to be spending your money?
- Are your finances organized?
- Do you have any (unwanted) debt?

#### Career & Work
- How do you make your money?
- What do you do? What is your position? What is your role? Where do you work?
- Are you so good they can't ignore you?
- Does your career give you mastery, control, and purpose?
- Are you engaged?
- Are you well-positioned and networked within your industry?
- How stressful is your career/work?

#### Health & Fitness
- What do you typically eat? What is your current diet?
- Do you regularly eat fast food or make healthy home-cooked meals?
- Are you generally tired or alert?
- How often do you get sick?
- Do you exercise? What form? How often?
- How much do you sleep on average? Are you getting good quality sleep?
- How much do you weigh?
- What is your resting heart rate?
- Are there any health risks that run in your family? Are you taking adequate steps to address these?

#### Education & Skill Development
- What is your current education status? What do you know?
- Are you spending time learning new things and developing your mind?
- What are your talents and skills?
- How much have you read in the past year?
- What talents and skills have you recently developed, or plan to develop?

#### Social Life & Relationships
- How is your home life?
- How is your relationship with the members of your immediate and extended family?
- Are you a good friend?
- How many core friends do you have? How large are your social circles?
- Are you cared for, loved, and valued by others?
- Are your partner and you aware of each other's needs and desires?
- What clubs, organizations, and communities are you a member of?
- Do you have influence and status in your social circles? In society?

#### Emotions & Well-Being
- How do you feel about your life?
- Are you enjoying life?
- Are you generally optimistic or pessimistic?
- Are you generally positive or negative?
- Are you easily aware of your current emotional state?
- Are you quick to notice how your emotions are influencing your thoughts, actions, and decisions?
- Do you have a regular mindfulness practice?
- Do you practice self-compassion?

#### Character & Identity
- What identity do you have?
- Is your identity small? (i.e., do you over-identify with any single label or group?)
- What are your key strengths and weaknesses?
- What are your greatest virtues and vices?
- How do you rate your compassion, integrity, empathy, honor, self-discipline, self-awareness, responsibility, courage, focus, authenticity, confidence, persistence, effectiveness?

#### Productivity & Organization
- What productivity systems and tools do you use?
- Are you organized? What are your systems?
- What are your routines and rituals?
- Is there too much complexity in your life? Do you need to simplify?
- Are you doing any deep work?
- Are you busy due to a lack of priorities?
- Do you have a good workspace environment and setup?

#### Adventure & Creativity
- Are you experiencing what you want to experience in life?
- What fun things have you done lately? In general?
- What creative things have you done lately? In general?
- What are your hobbies? What do you do for fun?

## Hamming Questions

Ask these for each life area after the specific questions, and also for life as a whole. Inspired by Richard Hamming's practice of asking colleagues "What are the important problems of your field?" and then "Why aren't you working on them?"

For each area (and for your life overall):

1. **What is the most important problem here?**
2. **What is your biggest bottleneck?**
3. **What is most holding you back from achieving your full potential?**
4. **What single thing, if successfully performed, would have the biggest impact here?**

### Interviewer Notes

- Capture the Hamming answer as the `bottleneck` field for each area in the profile.
- If the user struggles, offer an example: "For instance, someone might say 'solve my bad sleep problems' for Health, because poor sleep affects everything else."
- These questions often surface meta-skills — things that improve multiple areas (e.g., exercise, fighting procrastination). Flag these for the goals section.

## Rating Scale Guidance

### Area Rating (Present Reality)

Use a 1-7 Likert scale for each life area:

| Rating | Label |
|--------|-------|
| 1 | Very bad |
| 2 | Bad |
| 3 | Somewhat bad |
| 4 | Neither bad nor good |
| 5 | Somewhat good |
| 6 | Good |
| 7 | Very good |

Ask: "On a scale of 1 to 7, where 1 is very bad and 7 is very good, how would you rate this area of your life right now?"

### Importance Rating (Focus Areas)

Use the same 1-7 scale for importance:

| Rating | Label |
|--------|-------|
| 1 | Not at all important to focus on |
| 7 | Critically important to focus on |

Ask: "On a scale of 1 to 7, how important is it for you to focus on this area over the next year?"

This is asked during Phase 3, not Phase 2. The gap between current rating and importance helps identify priority areas.

## Phase 3: Ideal Future & Goals

After completing the present reality snapshot, shift to forward-looking questions. The goal is to paint a picture of what the user's ideal life looks like, then extract concrete goals.

### Per-Area Ideal Future Questions

For each of the twelve areas, ask:

- What do you want this area to be like?
- What would be awesome?
- What would this look like if it were perfect?
- What is something you've always wanted to do here?
- What would your ideal life look like in this area?
- Are there any specific projects to finish or goals to accomplish?
- If you were to die in a year, what would you do here?
- If you were to die in 10 years, what would you do here?
- For the metrics in this area, what do you ideally want them to be?
- If this area were improved, what would that look like? Make it visually concrete.

### Interviewer Notes

- Capture responses as the `ideal` field for each area.
- Push for specificity. "Make more money" is too vague. "Increase income to $X" or "eliminate all debt" is concrete.
- Don't let the user fill this with idle wishes. Each ideal should be something they genuinely want to strive toward, not just "wouldn't it be nice."
- Ask for the **importance rating (1-7)** for each area during this phase.

### Yearly Theme

Ask: "If you had to pick a theme for the coming year — 'Year of ___' — what would it be?"

- Could be a specific accomplishment (finish school, run a marathon) or a general direction (learn about cognitive psychology, independence, health).
- If their life is in flux, suggest quarterly themes instead.
- Capture as `yearly_theme` in the profile.

### Major Goals Extraction

After the per-area ideal future discussion, extract concrete goals:

- "Looking across all the areas we discussed, what are the 3-5 most important things you want to accomplish in the next year?"
- For each goal, ask:
  - What does success look like? How would you measure it?
  - Which life areas does this goal touch?
  - What's the timeframe?
- Encourage at least one **meta-skill goal** — something that improves multiple areas (e.g., regular exercise, sleep improvement, fighting procrastination).
- Goals should be specific and measurable, not vague resolutions.

## Phase 4: Skills, Strengths & Habits

This section is supplementary and not part of the original 8760 Hours methodology. It captures how the user works, not just what they want — particularly valuable for advisory agents that need to understand work patterns and tendencies.

### Work Habits

- What does a typical productive day look like for you?
- What does a typical unproductive day look like?
- When are you at your best? Morning, afternoon, evening?
- How long can you sustain focused work before needing a break?
- What environment do you work best in? (quiet, music, coffee shop, home office)
- How do you prefer to learn new things? (reading, video, hands-on, teaching others)
- Do you work better with deadlines or open-ended timelines?
- Do you prefer working alone or collaboratively?

### Known Tendencies

- What do you tend to avoid or put off? Why?
- What do you naturally gravitate toward, even when you should be doing something else?
- What triggers your procrastination? (boredom, fear of failure, overwhelm, ambiguity, perfectionism)
- When you get stuck, what do you typically do? (push through, switch tasks, take a break, avoid)
- Are there patterns in what projects you start but don't finish?
- What kinds of tasks drain your energy the most?
- What kinds of tasks give you energy?

### Strengths & Skills

- What are you genuinely good at? What do others come to you for?
- What technical skills do you have? (programming languages, tools, platforms, etc.)
- What soft skills are your strongest? (communication, leadership, negotiation, empathy, etc.)
- What domains do you have deep expertise in?
- What skills have you been meaning to develop but haven't?
- What do you pick up quickly? What takes you longer?

### Interviewer Notes

- This section is about self-knowledge, not judgment. Frame questions neutrally.
- Avoidance patterns and procrastination triggers are especially valuable for do-stuff-helper's advisory agents — capture these in detail.
- If the user is uncertain about strengths, ask: "What would your closest friend or colleague say you're best at?"
- Capture results in the `skills_strengths_habits` section of the profile.

## Progressive Depth Guidance

The 8760 Hours methodology uses mind maps for branching, non-linear exploration. In a conversational interview, we replace mind maps with **progressive depth prompting**. This preserves the benefit of catching things you'd miss with linear questioning.

### How to Apply Progressive Depth

1. **Start broad.** Begin each area with an open-ended question: "How would you describe the current state of [area]?"

2. **Identify themes.** Listen for the 2-4 key themes or topics in the user's response.

3. **Offer to branch.** For each theme: "You mentioned [X] — would you like to expand on that, or shall we move on?" Let the user choose which branches to explore.

4. **Go deeper on selected branches.** Ask follow-up probes from the area-specific questions above. Use "Can you give me some examples?" or "What does that look like in practice?" to draw out concrete detail.

5. **Summarize before moving on.** Before leaving each area, summarize what you captured: "Here's what I have for [area]: [summary]. Does that feel complete, or is there anything to add?"

6. **Don't force completeness.** Some areas will have rich detail; others will be sparse. That's fine. The user can always update the profile later.

### Pacing

- A full interview across all four phases can take 1-2 hours.
- It is better to do fewer areas well than to rush through all twelve.
- If the user is fatiguing, suggest saving progress and resuming later. The skill supports session continuity via a draft file.
- Aim for natural conversation, not rapid-fire Q&A. Give the user space to think.
